Yes, in a limited laboratory/near-field sense, but not in the “vast distance, wirelessly target one person’s brain cells and make them hallucinate speech/visions” sense.

The best-established effect is the microwave auditory effect, also called the Frey effect. Brief, intense pulses of radiofrequency/microwave energy absorbed in the head can make a person perceive clicks, buzzes, knocks, or other simple sounds. The leading explanation is not “direct mind control of neurons,” but rapid microscopic heating that creates pressure waves inside head tissues, which are then detected by the normal auditory system. A 2021 review concluded that systems could in principle create startling auditory sensations, but the equipment would be large, conspicuous, and constrained by power/safety limits.

What is not established is remote induction of rich, personalized auditory hallucinations such as clear voices, sentences, or “messages” from far away. Encoding intelligible words by microwave hearing is much harder than making clicks or tones, because the auditory system is not being fed a normal acoustic waveform with speaker-like fidelity. Claims that distant transmitters can selectively beam complex speech into a chosen person’s thoughts are not supported by mainstream evidence.

Other real brain-stimulation technologies also do not support the “vast distance” idea. TMS can noninvasively induce electric fields in cortex, but it uses a powerful coil placed very close to the scalp; even then, targeting depth and focality are major limits. Reviews describe TMS as inducing fields locally in superficial brain tissue, and deep targets require tradeoffs that quickly run into safety and focality problems.

Wireless electromagnetic energy also spreads, attenuates, reflects, and is absorbed by intervening space, buildings, skin, skull, and tissue. To affect neurons directly from a vast distance with precision comparable to implanted electrodes or near-scalp stimulation would require either enormous field strengths, obvious infrastructure, unsafe exposure levels, or a receiver implanted in or near the body. Without such a receiver, “brain-cell-specific” long-range targeting is not physically plausible with known public technology.

Simple perceived sounds from pulsed microwaves: physically real under certain exposure conditions.

Remote, covert, long-distance induction of speech-like auditory hallucinations or visual hallucinations in selected individuals by targeting brain cells: not supported by credible evidence and not plausible with known physics/neuroscience.
