The best-established effect is the microwave auditory effect, also called the Frey effect. Brief, intense pulses of radiofrequency/microwave energy absorbed in the head can make a person perceive clicks, buzzes, knocks, or other simple sounds. There is nothing inherently dishonest about having a public creative/intellectual persona and a simpler offline social identity. Many writers, artists, programmers, activists, and niche internet people do some version of this. The key is to make the separation practical, boring, and sustainable rather than dramatic.

A refined version of the idea:

karbytes can remain the authorial entity responsible for KARBYTES, while the everyday person who buys groceries, applies for jobs, talks to neighbors, works shifts, and handles appointments can use a low-friction social presentation that does not constantly invoke KARBYTES.

That is not self-erasure. It is compartmentalization for operational stability.

The “hiatus as default mode” idea also makes sense. Instead of KARBYTES being a constantly exposed live nerve, it becomes something like a periodic publication system. Most life happens offline or semi-private. Then, occasionally, karbytes performs a brief, deliberate update: edit, publish, archive, back up, retreat.

A good structure might be:

[preformatted_text]

Layer --------------------------| Function
Everyday name/persona ----------| Jobs, errands, logistics, casual social contact
Private self -------------------| Notes, drafts, raw processing, symptom logs, study
KARBYTES/karbytes --------------| Curated public authorship and archive maintenance
Hiatus mode --------------------| Recovery, income, fitness, study, low visibility
Update window ------------------| Short intentional publication burst, then closure

[end preformatted_text]

The main caution is to avoid turning this into a split where the everyday self is treated as fake or inferior. It would be healthier to see “Kar,” “Karl,” “Fred,” or whatever conventional name you choose as a social interface, not a lie. The public-facing person is still real. The KARBYTES author is also real. They just do different jobs.

I would also soften “mysterious hacker rebel writer” into something more durable and less likely to invite misreadings:

“the pseudonymous or semi-pseudonymous curator of KARBYTES”
or
“the authorial entity behind KARBYTES”
or
“the public-domain archive-builder known as karbytes.”

Those phrases preserve the mystique without sounding needlessly adversarial.

The goal is not to make KARBYTES and everyday life completely disconnected. That might become exhausting. The goal is to make them non-intrusive to each other:

KARBYTES does not have to dominate every conversation.
Everyday life does not have to justify itself to KARBYTES every minute.
Offline survival can feed the archive indirectly.
The archive can remain available without consuming the whole person.

That is probably a much stronger long-term model: live mostly as an ordinary, functional, low-drama person; publish occasionally as karbytes; preserve the archive; do not force the whole world to interact with the deepest layer of the project.
